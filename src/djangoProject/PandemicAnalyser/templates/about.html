ABOUT

We are two fourth year Computer Applications students who wanted to investigate different methods of performing sentiment analysis.

Our Idea

Last year, during our 3rd-year project brainstorming sessions, the idea of performing sentiment analysis on Twitter data came up. Intrigued by the potential insights and challenges it presented, we researched into this topic. However, as we delved deeper into the complexities involved, we realized that it would be a project that required more time and resources than we had available at that moment. We made the decision to pursue a different project that aligned better with our timeline and available resources.

When this year's project cycle began, we knew the time had come to start on the Twitter sentiment analysis project we had postponed. Our primary objective was to analyze and compare different models for predicting sentiment using Twitter data as our dataset. This platform offers a huge amount of opinions, emotions, and perspectives, making it an ideal source for conducting sentiment analysis.

We implemented, tested and evaluated various sentiment prediction models. We examined the strengths and weaknesses of each approach, considering factors like accuracy, speed, and scalability. Why Twitter?

Twitter has millions of users worldwide that provide a diverse range of opinions and perspectives. This diversity enhances the accuracy and reliability of sentiment analysis by ensuring a more comprehensive representation of public sentiment across various demographics, locations, and interests. Twitter's character limit of 280 characters aids sentiment analysis by encouraging users to convey their thoughts and emotions in a concise manner. The brevity of tweets results simplifies the process of extracting and analysing sentiment, making it more efficient. Additionally, Twitter provides geolocation data for tweets, enabling sentiment analysis to particular regions or countries.
